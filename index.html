Frida Kahlo

Disability: Polio, severe injuries from a bus accident

Kahlo transformed her pain into powerful art. Despite her physical challenges, she used self-portraiture to explore her identity and emotions, showcasing resilience in her vibrant, expressive style.

"I paint myself because I am so often alone and because I am the subject I know best." — Frida Kahlo

Vincent van Gogh

Disability: Mental health issues (depression, psychosis)

Van Gogh channeled his emotional turmoil into his art, using it as a form of expression. His unique style and bold colors reflect his inner struggles and determination to create beauty despite his challenges.

"I dream my painting and I paint my dream." — Vincent van Gogh

Chuck Close

Disability: Spinal artery collapse (paralyzed from the waist down)

Close adapted his painting techniques, using a specialized brush strapped to his wrist to create large-scale portraits. His innovative approach showcases how he turned adversity into an opportunity for artistic growth.

"Inspiration is for amateurs. The rest of us just show up and get to work." — Chuck Close

Georgia O'Keeffe

Disability: Macular degeneration

Even as her vision deteriorated, O'Keeffe continued to paint, relying on her intuition and memory. She adapted her techniques to convey her artistic vision, proving that passion can transcend physical limitations.

"I found I could say things with color and shapes that I couldn't say any other way." — Georgia O'Keeffe

Henry Darger

Disability: Mental health issues

Darger created intricate narratives that reflected his unique perspective. His imaginative storytelling served as a coping mechanism, allowing him to navigate his challenges through art.

"Art is not a thing; it is a way." — Henry Darger

Riva Lehrer

Disability: Physical disability

Lehrer explores identity and body image in her work, using her own experiences to challenge societal norms. Her art highlights the beauty of diverse bodies and the strength found in embracing one’s identity.

"The body is not just a vessel; it is part of who we are." — Riva Lehrer

Yayoi Kusama

Disability: Obsessive-compulsive disorder, hallucinations

Kusama’s art reflects her mental health experiences. She uses repetition and immersive installations to process her feelings, turning her challenges into a vibrant exploration of infinity and self-identity.

"The only thing I ever wanted to do was to make art." — Yayoi Kusama
